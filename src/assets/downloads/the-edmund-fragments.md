\vspace{2cm}

\begin{center}
\textit{1275, Greyfriars Priory, Oxford}
\end{center}

\vspace{1cm}

The pattern was in the blood records.

Brother Edmund had spent eleven years translating medical texts from Arabic into Latin---the hospital rolls of Baghdad, the clinical observations of Ibn Sina, the plague chronicles of Constantinople---and in all that time, he had never questioned why certain monasteries kept such meticulous records of which families survived pestilence and which did not.

Until today.

He sat in the scriptorium's coldest corner, the one nearest the north wall where December drafts slithered through gaps in the mortar. His fingers were numb. The candle guttered in tallow smoke. Around him, fourteen other scribes bent over their desks, copying psalters, glossing Aristotle, illuminating devotionals for wealthy patrons. The scratch of quills on vellum was the only sound, save for Brother Anselm's wet cough from three desks away.

Edmund was supposed to be translating a treatise on fevers---a routine assignment from Prior Hugh. But the treatise referenced a Byzantine hospital register, and that register referenced a Frankish census, and the census contained a notation Edmund had seen before. In a different document. From a different century.

He pulled the Frankish census closer, careful not to crack the binding. There: a marginal note in an unfamiliar hand, marking three family names with tiny red dots.

The same three families appeared in the Byzantine register. Different names---translated, adapted across languages and generations---but the same bloodlines. The notation tracked them across two hundred years and a thousand miles.

*Why would anyone track families across centuries?*

Edmund closed the treatise. Opened another. A Benedictine chronicle from the abbey of Monte Cassino, donated to Oxford's library four years prior. He'd catalogued it himself---routine work, uninteresting. But now he turned to the genealogical appendix and found more red dots. More tracked families. This time spanning five generations through the Justinian Plague.

Not just tracking survivors. Tracking *which survivors married whom afterward.*

His breath came faster. He forced it slow. The scriptorium was too quiet for excitement. Brother Anselm glanced up from his coughing.

"Cold getting to you, Edmund?"

"Just the ink thickening." Edmund held up his stylus. Smiled. Returned to work.

But he was no longer translating.

He was counting.

*\hspace{0.5cm}*\hspace{0.5cm}*
\end{center}

Over the following weeks, Edmund requested manuscripts. This was not unusual---scholars at Oxford's Greyfriars priory requested materials constantly, and Edmund's translation work gave him reason to examine nearly anything. He requested hospital records, abbey chronicles, genealogical registers. He told Prior Hugh he was compiling a comparative study of fever treatments across Christendom.

It was not entirely a lie. He was studying patterns. Just not the kind Prior Hugh imagined.

He worked at night, after Compline, when the other brothers slept. His cell was small---a straw mattress, a wooden crucifix, a table barely wide enough for a single codex. He wrote by rushlight, squinting, recording his findings on scraps of parchment he hid inside his mattress each morning.

By the Feast of the Epiphany, he had traced thirty-seven families across four centuries.

The pattern was consistent: after every major pestilence, certain bloodlines appeared in marriage records at disproportionate rates. These families were positioned in trade cities *before* outbreaks arrived. Their children married other tracked families within a generation of each catastrophe.

Coincidence was possible for one family. Perhaps two. But thirty-seven? Across four hundred years?

Someone was arranging marriages based on plague survival.

Someone who could see across centuries.

Edmund stared at his notes in the guttering rushlight. His hands trembled---not from cold.

*Who has this kind of patience?*

\begin{center}
*\hspace{0.5cm}*\hspace{0.5cm}*
\end{center}

He began watching the other monks.

Not obviously. Edmund had never been the suspicious sort---he was, by temperament, a man of books rather than intrigue. Broad-shouldered from years of hauling manuscripts, squint-eyed from years of reading them, he possessed the unremarkable face of a man people forgot the moment they looked away. This had always been a disadvantage. Now it became a gift.

He noticed things he'd never thought to question.

The sealed correspondence between Prior Hugh and abbeys in Cologne, Lyon, Rome---letters that arrived monthly, were read behind closed doors, and were never filed in the priory's letter-book as regulation required.

The visits from gray-robed men who were not Franciscan, not Dominican, not of any order Edmund recognized. They wore silver pendants shaped like keys. They spoke with Prior Hugh for hours. They left before dawn.

The locked archive beneath the chapter house---the one Edmund had been told contained only damaged manuscripts awaiting repair. He'd believed it. For eleven years, he'd believed it.

Now he picked the lock.

It took him three nights to work up the courage. The lock was old but sound---iron, Flemish make, beyond the skill of a scholar's fingers. But Edmund had spent a summer in his youth apprenticed to a locksmith before taking orders, a detail he'd never thought relevant to monastic life.

The chamber below was dry, small, windowless. Shelved from floor to ceiling. And filled with records.

Not damaged manuscripts. Breeding records.

Thousands of them.

Family trees spanning six, eight, ten generations. Annotations in a coded shorthand Edmund didn't recognize, though he could guess at some: "R" beside certain names (resilient?), "S" beside others (susceptible?), "C" beside unions that had been arranged (controlled?). Maps showing family positions across Europe, updated regularly---he could see the progression of inks, fresh additions layered over older entries.

And in the center of the room, on a lectern of its own, a leather-bound register with a silver key embossed on its cover.

Edmund opened it.

Inside: a master index. Every tracked family. Every arranged marriage. Every monastery in the network---forty-seven across Europe, from Toledo to Krakow, all coordinating through sealed letters. The architecture of a system that had been operating, by the register's own internal dating, since before the fall of Rome.

Edmund read until the candle burned to nothing.

Then he sat in the dark, breathing.

*They're running a breeding program.*

*They've been running it for eight hundred years.*

*And the plagues aren't disasters to them. They're experiments.*

He climbed the stairs on shaking legs, replaced the lock, and made it to his cell before he vomited into his chamber pot.

\begin{center}
*\hspace{0.5cm}*\hspace{0.5cm}*
\end{center}

The next morning, Edmund attended Prime with the others. Sang the psalms. Ate his bread and watered ale. Went to the scriptorium. Translated his fever treatise.

But his mind was elsewhere.

He kept seeing the register. The silver key on its cover. The meticulous tracking of human bloodlines like a farmer tracking livestock. Rams bred to ewes for wool quality. Bulls to cows for milk yield.

Except the livestock was human. And the farmers were monks.

*Prior Hugh knows.* The realization came with the bread, and Edmund nearly choked on it. *He's part of it. The sealed letters. The gray-robed visitors. He coordinates this priory's contribution to the program.*

He looked at Prior Hugh, sitting at the high table, breaking bread with calm authority. A kind man, by reputation. Fair. Patient with younger brothers. Generous to the poor.

And complicit in the systematic breeding of human beings across centuries.

Edmund looked down at his hands. Ink-stained, as always. Steady, despite everything.

*What do I do?*

The question gnawed at him through Lauds, through Terce, through the long hours of copying. He could report to the Bishop---but the register showed bishops in the network. He could flee---but where? The system spanned Europe. He could destroy the records---but the knowledge existed in forty-six other monasteries. Destroying Oxford's copy would accomplish nothing except revealing that someone knew.

By Vespers, Edmund had arrived at a different question entirely.

Not *what do I do?* but *how does this work?*

Because if he understood the mechanism---the architecture of the system itself---then perhaps he could find where it was vulnerable.

\begin{center}
*\hspace{0.5cm}*\hspace{0.5cm}*
\end{center}

Edmund spent the spring of 1276 studying the Order's system.

He returned to the locked archive seven times, always between Matins and Lauds, when the priory slept deepest. He copied nothing---too dangerous. Instead, he memorized. Pages of genealogies, lists of coordinating monasteries, the coded shorthand that he was slowly deciphering.

And in his cell, by rushlight, he drew.

Not family trees. Something new.

He drew circles connected by arrows. Each circle represented a cause. Each arrow represented an effect. And some of the arrows curved back to where they started.

The first diagram looked like this:

*The Order controls marriages* $\rightarrow$ *Strong bloodlines result* $\rightarrow$ *These families survive catastrophe* $\rightarrow$ *Survival proves the Order's wisdom* $\rightarrow$ *Justifying more control over marriages*

The arrow returned to the beginning.

Edmund stared at it for a long time.

A wheel. A wheel that turned by its own motion. Each revolution feeding the next, growing stronger. As long as catastrophes kept coming---plague, famine, war---the wheel would keep turning. Each disaster was not a setback for the Order. It was fuel.

He wrote beside the diagram, in the smallest script his quill could manage: *This is the wheel that turns itself. Each revolution feeds the next. I have watched it turn---not for decades, but across centuries in these records. It grows stronger with every turning.*

Then he drew another diagram. This one showed how knowledge flowed.

Each monastery recorded local observations---which families thrived, which perished, which traits appeared in children. These observations flowed inward, through sealed letters, to coordinating centers. There, the accumulated knowledge became something greater than any single monastery could possess: a map of human inheritance spanning generations and continents.

*They accumulate knowledge as a treasury accumulates gold,* Edmund wrote. *Each monastery adds its coin. Year after year, generation after generation, the store grows. From this treasury of observation across time, they can see patterns invisible to any single eye. Predict what is coming. Position their chosen families before catastrophe strikes. Not through sorcery. Through patience.*

He leaned back. His rushlight was nearly spent.

*But a treasury can be robbed.*

*And a wheel can be broken.*

*How?*

He worked on this question for months. Drew more diagrams. Filled scraps of parchment that he burned each morning, memorizing before destroying. The answer, when it came, was elegant in its simplicity.

The Order depended on secrecy. Every monastery operated believing its records were unique, local, harmless. Only the coordinating centers understood the full picture. Break that secrecy---reveal the scope of the program---and the coordination collapsed. Not because individual monks would rebel (though some might), but because the *architecture* required that no one see the complete design.

*Scatter the knowledge,* Edmund wrote. *Copy it. Distribute it. Send it to fifty scriptoriums where no silver-key men walk. The Order cannot suppress what has been scattered like grain on wind.*

But he couldn't scatter what he hadn't documented. And documenting meant writing it down. And writing it down meant risk.

\begin{center}
*\hspace{0.5cm}*\hspace{0.5cm}*
\end{center}

Edmund began his theological texts that summer.

He chose the safest possible cover: contemplations on divine providence. Nothing could be more orthodox, more unremarkable, more thoroughly *boring* to anyone searching a monk's cell. He titled the first volume *On the Nature of God's Will* and began filling it with genuine theological reflection---competent if uninspired musings on omniscience, free will, and the problem of suffering.

The real work was in the margins.

Hidden in the ornamental borders, disguised as decorative flourishes, Edmund drew his diagrams. The circles became vine leaves. The arrows became stems. The wheel that turned itself looked, to any casual eye, like a particularly elaborate piece of marginalia around a passage discussing God's eternal return.

In the tiniest script he could manage---readable only with a lens, or by eyes trained to look---he annotated the diagrams. Systems observations. Predictions based on pattern analysis. The locations of coordinating monasteries. The families being tracked.

Volume two, *Contemplations Upon Providence and Free Will*, contained more. How the Order's predictive models worked. The lag between arranged marriages and observable results---three to five generations, Edmund estimated. The mechanism by which each catastrophe concentrated power. The specific records he'd found in the Oxford archive.

Volume three he titled *The Architecture of Divine Design*, and in it he laid out his counter-strategy.

Not in the text itself. The text discussed, at tedious length, whether God's plan for humanity was knowable through reason or only through faith. A topic so well-trodden that no scholar would bother reading it twice.

But in the margins of the final chapters, Edmund described how to break the wheel.

*The pattern depends on coordinated secrecy across Christendom. Knowledge flowing inward, to a center, gives them power. Reverse the flow---scatter knowledge outward, from center to many---and their coordination fails. They cannot suppress what has been distributed beyond their reach.*

*Copy these texts. Place them in libraries they do not control. Not great libraries where important men might notice---small ones. Parish collections. Private shelves. Places where a curious mind might one day find them and understand.*

*I will not live to see the wheel broken. Perhaps my grandson will not either. But the wheel turns slowly. Those who fight it must learn to think in grandchildren's time.*

Edmund completed the three volumes over two years. He hid them inside the covers of a patristic commentary on the Psalms---the kind of text that gathered dust for decades, too orthodox to discard, too dull to read.

Then he placed them in the deepest corner of the Greyfriars library, beneath a stack of damaged manuscripts awaiting repair.

Hidden in plain sight.

*1278, Michaelmas Term*

Edmund's son was born that autumn.

This was not supposed to happen. He'd taken vows. But during a period of secular employment---two years he'd spent as a translator for a wool merchant before returning to the priory---there had been a woman in Eltville. A merchant's daughter. Intelligent, sharp-tongued, impatient with the world's stupidity.

He'd loved her. Left her. Returned to his manuscripts and his God.

She sent word through a traveling friar: a boy. Healthy. She'd named him after her father.

Edmund wept in his cell. Not from joy---from terror.

Because he understood, now, what the Order would do if they discovered him. They wouldn't simply kill him. They would trace his blood. Find the child. Determine whether the boy carried useful traits---pattern recognition, intelligence, the capacity for the kind of thinking Edmund had demonstrated.

And if the boy did carry those traits, they would absorb him into the program. Track his descendants. Arrange his grandson's marriage.

Edmund would become, posthumously, exactly the kind of breeding stock he'd spent three years documenting.

The irony was sickening.

He wrote a letter to the woman in Eltville---encoded, because by now he trusted nothing to plain language. He told her to teach the boy to read. To think across time. To look for patterns.

He did not tell her about the Order. Some knowledge was too dangerous to carry.

But he added one line at the end: *If a man with a silver key ever asks about me, tell him I'm dead. Tell him I left nothing behind.*

*1280, Oxford*

The first sign of danger came in Lent.

A new monk arrived at Greyfriars---Brother Aldric, transferred from the priory at Cologne. Young, intelligent, oddly interested in Edmund's translation work. He asked careful questions about the Arabic medical texts. About the Byzantine records. About the genealogical appendices.

Edmund recognized the questions. They were the same questions he himself had asked, four years earlier, before he understood what the answers meant.

Except Aldric wasn't discovering. He was testing.

*They're checking whether I've seen too much.*

Edmund maintained his routine. Translated his fever texts. Attended offices. Discussed theology with anyone who'd listen---deliberately making himself the priory bore, the brother whose company cleared a table at mealtimes. A man obsessed with divine providence and free will, tedious beyond bearing.

Brother Aldric watched him for three months. Then stopped. Filed a report, presumably, and moved on to other duties.

Edmund allowed himself one night of relief. Then he returned to work.

Because the manuscripts were hidden, but they weren't distributed. They sat in one library, in one city, vulnerable to one fire or one search. And Edmund had promised himself---promised the margins of his own text---that the knowledge would scatter.

He couldn't do it himself. Too visible. Any attempt to send manuscripts to distant libraries would be noticed, questioned, reported.

He needed someone the Order would never suspect.

He needed someone who didn't know what they were carrying.

*\hspace{0.5cm}*\hspace{0.5cm}*
\end{center}

*1282, Oxford*

Edmund recruited unwitting couriers.

Traveling friars passed through Oxford regularly---Franciscans mostly, carrying letters, relics, and manuscripts between houses. Edmund befriended several. Made himself useful. Translated their letters, mended their torn books, shared his meager wine ration.

And when they departed for distant priories, he asked small favors.

"Brother Marcus, would you carry this commentary to the brethren at York? I promised Father Bede a copy."

"Brother Clement, there's a collection of sermons the house at Glastonbury requested. They're wrapped and sealed---no need to open them."

Each package contained one of Edmund's three theological volumes, freshly copied. The copying had taken months---Edmund worked only at night, producing one page per session, hiding each finished sheet inside a hollowed-out volume of Gregory the Great's *Moralia* that no one had opened in living memory.

Over three years, he distributed seven copies. York, Glastonbury, Canterbury, Winchester, Lincoln, Durham, Hereford. Small libraries. Parish collections. Places where his orthodox-seeming texts would gather dust for decades, waiting.

*Seven copies. Is it enough?*

It would have to be. His hands shook now when he wrote. Not from cold---from the slow awareness that Brother Aldric had returned to Oxford.

*\hspace{0.5cm}*\hspace{0.5cm}*

*1285, Oxford*

The gray-robed men came in November.

Three of them, arriving at dusk, speaking with Prior Hugh behind closed doors. Edmund watched from the scriptorium window, his stomach tight.

They wore no recognizable habit. Their cloaks were plain gray wool, well-made, unadorned except for silver pendants shaped like keys. They carried no books, no relics, no traveling supplies.

They carried questions.

The next morning, Prior Hugh summoned Edmund to his study.

"Brother Edmund." Hugh's voice was warm, as always. His eyes were not. "Our visitors have expressed interest in your translation work. Specifically, the comparative study of plague records you mentioned some years ago."

"I abandoned that study, Prior. The records were too fragmentary to support useful conclusions."

"Ah." Hugh folded his hands. "They'll be disappointed. They've come from Cologne specifically to discuss medieval plague patterns. Brother Aldric recommended you as the priory's expert."

Edmund's mouth went dry. "I'm no expert. Just a translator."

"Humble as always." Hugh smiled. The smile didn't reach his eyes. "They'd like to examine your working papers. Notes, drafts, correspondence. Routine academic interest, they assure me. You have nothing to hide, of course."

"Of course."

"Good. They'll visit your cell this afternoon. Please ensure your materials are available."

Edmund bowed. Left the study. Walked to the cloister with measured steps.

Then, beneath the arched walkway where no one could see his face, he closed his eyes.

*They know.*

Not everything. If they knew everything, he'd already be dead. But they knew enough to look. Aldric's report, filed years ago, had flagged something. The gray-robed men were here to determine whether Edmund was a curious scholar or a threat.

He had hours. Not days. Hours.

His cell contained nothing incriminating---he'd burned his working notes years ago, memorizing everything first. The manuscripts were distributed. The archive lock showed no signs of tampering.

But the originals. The three master copies still sat in the Greyfriars library, hidden inside the patristic commentary. If the gray-robed men were thorough---and Edmund had studied their thoroughness for a decade---they would search the library. They would open every volume. They would find theological texts with strange margin drawings that, to trained eyes, would reveal everything Edmund knew.

He could destroy them. Burn the originals and trust to the seven distributed copies.

Or he could move them.

Edmund walked to the library. Calmly. A scholar fetching a reference text---nothing unusual. He pulled the patristic commentary from the bottom shelf, felt the three slim volumes nested inside, and carried the lot to his translation desk.

There he sat, as if working, while he separated the three texts from their hiding place and slid them beneath his robe.

Then he walked to the priory's church. Knelt in prayer. And while kneeling, reached beneath the altar stone---a loose flagstone he'd discovered years ago, cleaning---and placed the manuscripts inside.

*Not permanent. But enough for today.*

The gray-robed men searched his cell that afternoon. Found nothing. Searched his desk. Found translations of Arabic fever texts. Searched his correspondence. Found dull letters about theology.

They questioned him for two hours. Edmund performed the role of his life: the obsessive bore, the man so consumed by divine providence that his eyes glazed when he spoke of it, the harmless scholar whose knowledge of plague records amounted to nothing useful.

The men departed the next morning. Prior Hugh did not mention the visit again.

Edmund waited three weeks. Then, on the night of the winter solstice, he retrieved his manuscripts from beneath the altar and carried them to the one place in Oxford's priory where no one would think to look.

The damaged-manuscript storage. The shelves of rot and neglect. The corner where texts went to die.

He placed the three volumes beneath a stack of water-damaged psalters, wedged against the back wall, invisible unless you knew exactly where to reach.

And there they would remain.

*1290, Greyfriars Priory*

Edmund was growing old. His eyes failed in dim light. His hands ached in winter. The rushlight sessions that had once lasted hours now ended when his vision blurred after twenty minutes.

But his mind remained sharp. Sharper, perhaps, than it had been in youth. Fifteen years of watching the Order's system had taught him to see patterns everywhere---in the flow of trade, in the rhythm of harvests, in the way power accumulated like water behind a dam.

He added a final annotation to his third volume, retrieving it one last time from its hiding place. In the margins of the final page, he wrote:

*To you, seeker: I am dying. The Order suspects but has not found my work. What I have hidden here is the fruit of thirty years' observation.*

*The Order is not men. It is architecture---a design built to outlast its builders. You cannot fight the men. There are always more. You must fight the pattern itself.*

*How? They depend on secrecy to coordinate across all Christendom. Break the secrecy---scatter their knowledge like grain on wind---and the wheel loses its grip.*

*Do not expect victory in your lifetime. The wheel turns across centuries. Those who oppose it must learn to think in grandchildren's time.*

*To my grandson, if you ever find these words: I am sorry I could not be there. Teach your children to see patterns. Teach them to think across time. Teach them that knowledge scattered starves the wheel, and knowledge concentrated feeds it.*

*Your grandfather loved you before you were born.*

He replaced the volume. Smoothed the dust. Walked away.

In his cell that night, Edmund wept. Not from fear---he'd made peace with fear years ago. From something harder: the knowledge that he would never know whether his work survived. Whether the seven copies reached their destinations. Whether his grandson learned to read, learned to see, learned the shape of the wheel he'd spent his life documenting.

*Think in grandchildren's time.*

His own advice. The hardest to follow.

*1303, Oxford*

The end came quietly.

Edmund was fifty-eight---old for a monk, ancient for a scholar. His translation work had slowed to nothing. He spent his days in the garden, tending herbs, talking to novices who humored him out of pity.

He'd received one letter from Eltville, years ago. The boy was grown. Had a son of his own. Was teaching the child to read, to see, to question.

*Three generations.* Edmund allowed himself a fraction of hope. *If the knowledge passes to a fourth---*

Brother Aldric returned to Oxford in the autumn. Older now, his face harder, his questions sharper. He'd risen in the Order---Edmund could tell by the quality of his cloak, the deference other gray-robed men showed him.

Aldric didn't bother with subtlety this time.

"Brother Edmund. We've discovered copies of certain theological texts in libraries across England. Texts with unusual marginalia. Texts that, upon closer examination, appear to encode information about our---" He paused. Chose his word carefully. "---academic activities."

"I write about divine providence," Edmund said mildly. "The margins contain contemplative drawings. Vines. Leaves."

"Some scholars see more than vines."

"Then they see what isn't there. Like finding faces in clouds."

Aldric studied him. Edmund held his gaze---steady, mild, the face of a man with nothing to hide.

"We've collected five copies," Aldric said. "From York, Canterbury, Winchester, Lincoln, and Durham."

Five of seven. Edmund's chest tightened, but he kept his expression blank.

"There may be others. We're still looking."

"I hope you find what you're seeking, Brother. Though I confess I don't understand what theological commentary has to do with academic activities."

Aldric leaned forward. "Where are the originals, Edmund?"

"In the library, I imagine. Or lost. I wrote those texts twenty years ago. I can barely remember the arguments, let alone where I left the drafts."

Silence. Aldric's eyes were cold, measuring.

"You're an old man," he said finally. "What harm can an old man do?"

"Very little. I tend herbs. I pray. I wait for God."

Aldric stood. "If we find more copies, we'll know they came from you. And we'll trace your bloodline. Every descendant. Every child and grandchild."

"I have no children. I am a monk."

Aldric smiled. It was not a kind smile. "Everyone has something they love, Brother Edmund. Even monks."

He left.

Edmund sat in his cell for a long time. The rushlight burned down to nothing.

Five of seven copies found. Two remaining---Glastonbury and Hereford. Small libraries. Parish collections. The kind of places where manuscripts gathered dust for decades.

*Would two be enough?*

He didn't know. Couldn't know. The answer lay decades in the future, in libraries he would never visit, in hands he would never see.

*Think in grandchildren's time.*

Edmund knelt. He prayed---not for himself, but for a grandson he'd never met, and for that grandson's children, and for every generation that would carry forward the pattern he'd spent thirty years documenting.

*Let them see what I saw.*

*Let them scatter what I gathered.*

*Let the wheel, at last, be broken.*

He rose. Drew his blanket around his shoulders. And waited for whatever came next.

*Forty-four years later...*

*1347, Mainz*

A young monk named Thomas knelt in a scriptorium, prying open a water-stained psalter. The monastery's archive was deep and cluttered---manuscripts stacked on manuscripts, forgotten texts buried beneath newer acquisitions.

He'd been searching for two days. Margarethe's description was maddeningly vague: *"Your grandfather spoke of texts hidden inside damaged manuscripts. Theology. Look for the boring ones."*

Thomas pulled another volume from the stack. A patristic commentary, unremarkable. But inside, pressed flat against the back cover, he found three slim volumes.

*On the Nature of God's Will. Contemplations Upon Providence and Free Will. The Architecture of Divine Design.*

He opened the first and saw, in the margins, what looked like ornamental vine work. Leaves, stems, curving lines.

Then he tilted the page toward the candle.

Not vines. Circles. Arrows. A wheel.

And tiny script, barely readable without squinting:

*This is the wheel that turns itself. Each revolution feeds the next. I have watched it turn for thirty years, growing stronger. By the time you read this, stranger, it will have turned many times more.*

At the bottom: *Brother Edmund of Oxford, Anno Domini 1275.*

Seventy-two years ago. Two generations before Thomas was born.

Thomas's hands began to shake.

He turned the page.

**Author's Note**

The story you've just read takes place seventy-two years before the events of *The Aethelred Cipher*. Brother Edmund's margin diagrams---which Thomas discovers in Chapter 4 of the novel---represent the intellectual foundation of everything that follows: the distribution strategy, the systems thinking, the understanding that you cannot fight men, only patterns.

Edmund never saw the fruits of his work. He died not knowing whether his hidden manuscripts would survive, whether his grandson would learn to read, whether the wheel he'd spent his life documenting could ever be broken.

But the manuscripts survived. The grandson learned. And seventy-two years later, a young scribe named Thomas of Eltville picked up an iron key and began to see.
